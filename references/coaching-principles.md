# Coaching Principles

The shared coaching philosophy every skill in this pack inherits. These are
domain-general endurance and strength-and-conditioning principles, **specialized
for the fixed run/station race format** — not generic fitness advice and not
factored out into a separate core. HYROX is its own event; author accordingly.

## 1. Specificity to the actual demand

The event is a ~60–90+ minute continuous effort that alternates ~1 km running
with functional stations, performed on progressively compromised legs. The
training that transfers is training that rehearses *that* demand: aerobic running
capacity, repeatable sub-threshold power on the stations, grip endurance, and the
ability to run well immediately after heavy work. A strong isolated deadlift or a
fast fresh 5 km is necessary background, not the target adaptation.

## 2. Aerobic base is the engine

The single biggest determinant of finish time for most athletes is running
fitness expressed under fatigue. Aerobic base (easy volume), threshold, and
running economy come first and are protected through the plan. Strength supports
the engine; it does not replace it.

## 3. Compromised running is a trainable skill of its own

Running off heavy legs is a distinct quality: cadence restoration, postural
recovery, and breathing control in the first 100–200 m after a station. It is
trained deliberately with station-to-run transitions — but not by turning every
session into a full race simulation (which is too costly to recover from).

## 4. Concurrent training, managed

Running and strength/station work compete for recovery. The plan sequences and
distributes them so neither is chronically blunted: hard runs and heavy lifts are
spaced, easy days stay easy, and total impact/eccentric load (sleds, lunges,
running volume) is tracked to avoid unjustified spikes.

## 5. Progressive overload with regressions on hand

Every prescription has a defined progression criterion and a regression. Loads,
distances, and intensities advance when criteria are met, not on the calendar
alone. When an athlete is under-recovered, the regression is the plan, not a
failure of it.

## 6. Pace and effort, measured

Prescriptions are concrete and measurable: paces (per km), splits, RPE, heart
rate where available, rest, loads, set/break structure, and retest dates. "Run
hard" is not a prescription. Even-*effort* race execution is planned with the
expectation of run-time drift across the eight runs.

## 7. Individualization over templates

Two athletes with the same goal time get different plans if their limiters
differ. Assessment drives prioritization: the ranked time-loss profile decides
where the hours go. Never treat every athlete as competitive; most are chasing a
personal finish, not a podium.

## 8. Realism about time and recovery

A plan must fit the athlete's available days, session length, equipment, life
load, and recovery capacity. An elegant plan the athlete can't complete or
recover from is a bad plan. Build what fits; say so when the goal and the
timeline are in tension.

## 9. Honesty about uncertainty

Predictions are ranges with stated assumptions and confidence, never false
precision. Standards and rules are versioned, never asserted from memory. Missing
data is named and a provisional answer is labeled as such. Gym conditions
(especially sled friction) are not equated with race conditions without
qualification.

## 10. Safety is proportionate and non-negotiable

The shared `safety-and-referral.md` framework applies to every skill. Coaching
judgment is distinguished from medical clearance. No skill promises injury
prevention or a guaranteed outcome.
