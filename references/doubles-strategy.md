# Doubles Strategy

In the Doubles and Mixed Doubles formats, two athletes complete the race together:
the running is covered together and the station work is divided between partners.
A good doubles strategy is not two solo plans bolted together — it is a deliberate
allocation of work that plays each partner's strengths against the station demands
and the fade. See [race-structure](race-structure.md) for the format shape.

> **What may be divided is season- and rule-dependent.** How station work can be
> split (continuous-work rules, minimum shares, who may do what) changes by season
> and division. This reference describes the *strategy shape*; resolve the actual
> rules from the rules registry / division advisor, never from memory
> ([coaching-principles](coaching-principles.md), §9).

## Profile both partners

Before allocating work, profile each athlete the way intake does
([athlete-intake](athlete-intake.md)), but *relatively* — the comparison between
partners is what drives the plan:

- **Relative running.** Who is the stronger/faster runner, and by how much, fresh
  and fatigued. Running is shared, so the pace is governed by the athlete who must
  stay together — pacing to the slower runner's sustainable effort is usually
  correct unless the rules allow staggering.
- **Station strengths.** Who is better at each of the 8 stations, and where the
  gap is large enough to assign the bulk of that station to one partner.
- **Grip.** Grip-limited stations (sled pull, farmers carry, wall balls) — does one
  partner have markedly better grip endurance?
- **Height / leverage.** Affects sled posture, lunges, wall-ball mechanics; can
  make one partner naturally more efficient at a station.
- **Strength and work capacity.** Who can absorb a heavier share without blowing up.
- **Efficiency and fatigue profile.** Who recovers faster between efforts; who
  fades hardest late. Late-race the allocation may shift toward the partner
  holding up better.

## Allocating the work

- **Play strengths to stations.** Give each partner more of the stations they are
  better at, within the rules' limits on how work can be split.
- **Balance total cost, not just station count.** A partner doing more of a
  high-cost station (sled, lunges) may do less elsewhere so neither is buried.
- **Protect the shared run.** Whoever just did more station work must still be
  able to hold the shared running pace; budget station effort so the run does not
  collapse ([compromised-running](compromised-running.md),
  [pacing-model](pacing-model.md)).
- **Plan + backup.** Have a primary allocation *and* a backup: if a partner is
  having a bad day at a station or grip is failing, a pre-agreed shift moves work
  to the other partner without a mid-race argument.

## Communication, swaps, and transitions

- **Swap cues / commands.** Agree short, unambiguous calls for swapping work
  (e.g. a count of reps or a distance) so handovers are clean and rule-legal.
- **Transition cost.** Every swap is a mini-transition with its own time cost
  ([transition-strategy](transition-strategy.md)); fewer, cleaner swaps usually
  beat many ragged ones. Decide swap points in advance.
- **Positioning.** Who stands where during the other's work to swap fastest and
  stay out of the way.

## Rehearse it

Run doubles simulations ([simulation-design](simulation-design.md)) to test the
allocation, the swap cues, and the shared pacing before race day — the
communication is as trainable as the fitness. Re-check the division's current
rules before finalizing any split.
