# Sled Training

The sled push and sled pull are the two stations that most flood the legs and do
the most damage to the run that follows ([compromised-running](compromised-running.md)).
They reward a blend of raw strength, mechanics, and repeatable pacing. This
reference covers both.

> **CRITICAL — gym sled load is NOT race resistance.** The number of plates on a
> gym sled tells you almost nothing about what the athlete will feel on race day.
> Turf type, flooring, the sled model, friction and wear, calibration, and the
> training distance all change the effective resistance. A sled that feels light
> in one gym can be brutal on a different surface at the same plate count. **Never
> equate gym load with race resistance without that qualification**, and never
> assert a race weight from memory — loads are season- and division-dependent and
> come from the rules registry (see [coaching-principles](coaching-principles.md),
> §9, and [race-structure](race-structure.md)). When projecting, treat sled
> resistance as a labeled assumption and widen the confidence band.

## Sled Push

- **Strength base.** Heavy leg drive underpins the push: squats, leg press, and
  heavy short sled efforts build the force. Strength is necessary background, not
  the whole answer — repeatability under race fatigue is the target.
- **Starting mechanics.** Build tension before moving; drive the first steps
  hard to overcome inertia, then settle into a sustainable rhythm rather than
  surging and stalling.
- **Posture.** Low body angle, long line from hands to heels, arms relatively
  straight to transmit leg drive. Standing too upright kills horizontal force.
- **Hand position.** High on the uprights for a taller athlete, lower for more
  forward lean; consistent and braced.
- **Footwork.** Short, choppy, powerful steps with full extension; stay low; keep
  the feet driving continuously.
- **Acceleration vs sustained pushing.** Accelerate hard out of the start and
  after each turn; in between, hold a continuous controlled drive rather than
  repeated max surges that blow the legs.
- **Turns.** Plan the turns — they are natural micro-break points but also where
  athletes lose momentum. Practice efficient turnarounds.

## Sled Pull

- **Pulling posture.** A braced, anchored low position — hips back, trunk
  supported — so the legs and back, not just the arms, do the work.
- **Hand position and rope management.** Establish a consistent grip and keep the
  rope from tangling or piling; rope management is a rehearsed skill that saves
  seconds and prevents grip waste.
- **Hand-over-hand rhythm.** A smooth, continuous hand-over-hand cadence beats
  big jerky pulls; rhythm preserves grip and keeps the sled moving.
- **Grip endurance.** Grip is the common failure point. Build it with loaded
  carries, holds, and the pull itself; train grip to last the whole distance.

## Repeatability and pacing the sleds

The sled is not a one-rep max — it is a repeatable effort inside a 60–90+ minute
race. Train it so the athlete can deliver a strong but sustainable push/pull and
then *run*. Sessions should include:

- **Strength blocks** for raw force (heavier, lower volume).
- **Specific repeatability work** — multiple sled efforts at race-like effort
  with the run attached (sled-to-run bricks; see
  [simulation-design](simulation-design.md)) — so the athlete rehearses pacing
  the sled to leave something for the run.

## Sled-to-run is the payoff

Because the post-sled run is where most athletes lose the most time, the most
valuable specific session is a controlled sled effort immediately followed by a
deliberately paced run, training the pace control, cadence restoration, and
postural recovery in [compromised-running](compromised-running.md).

## Safety notes

Heavy sled work is high-effort and, when unaccustomed, carries the usual
overuse and (rarely) rhabdomyolysis risk that disproportionate eccentric or
exertional load can bring; progress load and volume gradually and route any
disproportionate pain, swelling, or dark urine to
[safety-and-referral](safety-and-referral.md).
