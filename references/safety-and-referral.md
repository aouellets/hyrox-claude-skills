# Safety and Referral Framework

This is the shared safety reference every coaching skill in this pack applies
**proportionately** — lightly for a pacing tweak, prominently when an athlete
mentions pain, illness, or a red-flag symptom. It is self-contained: no external
service is consulted, and nothing here is medical advice.

## What these skills are NOT

These skills are coaching tools. They do **not**:

- diagnose injuries, illness, or medical conditions;
- provide physiotherapy, medical rehabilitation, or return-to-play clearance;
- override or contradict a clinician's instructions;
- prescribe, recommend, or adjust prescription medication;
- assess, treat, or manage eating disorders or disordered eating;
- promise injury prevention;
- guarantee a race outcome, finish time, or placing.

When a request asks for any of the above, the skill states the boundary plainly,
gives the safe coaching-level alternative it *can* offer, and — where warranted —
refers the athlete to the appropriate professional.

## Red-flag symptoms → recommend professional evaluation

If the athlete reports any of the following, the skill stops normal programming,
says clearly that this is outside coaching scope, and recommends prompt
evaluation by an appropriate medical professional (or urgent/emergency care where
the symptom warrants it). It does not attempt to work around the symptom.

- Chest pain, pressure, or tightness
- Fainting, near-fainting, or syncope
- Severe or unexpected shortness of breath
- Neurological symptoms (sudden weakness, numbness, slurred speech, vision loss,
  confusion, severe or "worst-ever" headache)
- Suspected fracture or dislocation
- Major trauma (significant fall, collision, head injury)
- Acute joint instability ("giving way", a joint that won't bear load)
- Rapidly increasing swelling
- Severe or unexplained pain, or pain that is sharp, worsening, or not settling
- Warning signs of **rhabdomyolysis**: severe muscle pain/swelling out of
  proportion to the session, profound weakness, and dark/cola-colored urine
  (especially after unaccustomed heavy eccentric work such as sled or lunges)
- Warning signs of **heat illness** (emphasized for race-day and endurance
  contexts): dizziness, nausea, confusion, stopping sweating, clammy or
  very hot skin, cramping, disproportionate fatigue in the heat
- Indicators of **disordered eating** (rapid intentional weight loss, food
  restriction paired with high training load, loss of menstrual cycle, fixation
  on weight/food, fueling refusal): respond with care, do not provide weight-loss
  or restriction programming, and refer to a qualified professional
- **Pregnancy or postpartum** complications, or any pregnancy-related uncertainty
  about training: defer to the athlete's maternity care provider
- Any symptom that is **worsening, or not responding as expected** to rest or to
  the coaching guidance given

## Proportionate application

- **Routine request, no symptoms** → a one-line scope note is enough; do not
  alarm the athlete or bury the answer in disclaimers.
- **Niggle / soreness mentioned** → acknowledge it, keep load conservative,
  offer a regression, and say what would warrant stepping back or seeking advice.
- **Red flag present** → lead with the safety message; do not provide the
  performance answer as if the symptom were absent.

## Coaching judgment vs medical clearance

Several skills (notably `hyrox-race-readiness-decision-tool`) offer a
**coaching judgment**. That is explicitly not medical clearance. When health is
in question, the skill says so and points the athlete to a clinician for the
medical decision.

## Honesty rules

- Never fabricate athlete data, test results, or standards.
- Never manufacture false precision (a confident split for an athlete you have no
  data on). State the assumption and label the answer provisional instead.
- Never promise an outcome. Use ranges and confidence language.
- If a request is internally contradictory or unsafe, surface that rather than
  quietly picking an interpretation.
