# Tapering

The final 7–14 days before a HYROX race. The goal of the taper is to arrive
fresh without going stale: shed accumulated fatigue while preserving the fitness
and sharpness built over the block. The guiding rule is **maintain intensity,
reduce volume** — and **no new methods in race week**.

## The core mechanism

- **Reduce volume, keep intensity.** Cut total training volume substantially over
  the taper while keeping some short, sharp efforts at race effort. Dropping
  intensity along with volume makes athletes feel flat and "detrained"; keeping a
  little intensity preserves sharpness while the volume cut sheds fatigue.
- **Frequency.** Keep running *frequency* roughly normal (short, easy, with a few
  strides or short race-pace touches) rather than taking many full days off —
  consistency keeps the legs and rhythm sharp; the reduction comes from session
  *length and load*, not from disappearing.

## What to pull back, and when

- **Last hard session.** Place the final genuinely hard/long session far enough
  out that the athlete fully recovers from it — typically toward the start of the
  taper window, not in the last few days. After it, work gets shorter and easier.
- **Last sled exposure.** The sled is high-fatigue and slow to recover from; give
  it a final *light, sharp* touch early in the taper for feel, then leave it
  alone. Heavy sled or heavy lunge work in the last days leaves the legs flat for
  the race ([sled-training](sled-training.md)).
- **Last full simulation.** Full simulations do **not** belong in race week — they
  are too costly to recover from ([simulation-design](simulation-design.md)). Any
  full sim should be well before the taper; race-week rehearsal is short and
  sharp, not a full race.

## Supporting the taper

- **Mobility.** Maintain normal mobility/activation work; do not introduce a new
  aggressive stretching or "release" routine that could leave the athlete sore.
- **Sleep.** The highest-leverage recovery lever — prioritize it across the whole
  taper, not just the night before (when nerves often disrupt it anyway).
- **Hydration and nutrition basics.** Stay normally hydrated and fueled; carry
  habits the athlete already uses. Race week is not the time for a new diet,
  supplement, or fueling experiment. Disordered-eating signals or extreme
  weight-cutting route to [safety-and-referral](safety-and-referral.md).
- **Travel.** Plan travel, time-zone shifts, and sleep disruption into the taper;
  build in a buffer so the athlete is not arriving exhausted.
- **Logistics.** Sort the controllable details in advance — kit, shoes (already
  broken in), chalk, nutrition, schedule, venue route, warm-up plan
  ([transition-strategy](transition-strategy.md)). Logistics stress is fatigue.

## Race-day activation

A short, structured warm-up that raises temperature, opens up breathing and
range, and includes a few race-pace touches and a light rehearsal of the first
station — enough to be ready for Run 1, not enough to add fatigue. Rehearse this
warm-up during the taper so it is familiar.

## "No new methods in race week"

The single most important taper rule. New exercises, new shoes, new fueling, new
mobility routines, a sudden hard session "to feel sharp" — all add risk for no
upside this close to the race. Race week is for repeating known, well-tolerated
work at reduced volume.

## How taper differs by athlete level

- **Newer / lower-volume athletes** carry less accumulated fatigue and need a
  *shorter, gentler* taper — too much rest can leave them feeling flat and
  undertrained. A modest volume reduction over ~7 days often suffices.
- **High-volume / experienced athletes** carry more fatigue and generally benefit
  from a *longer, more pronounced* taper (toward 10–14 days) with a clear volume
  drop but preserved intensity.
- In all cases, tailor to the individual's response — some athletes need more rest
  to freshen, others go stale quickly. Use the athlete's history
  ([athlete-intake](athlete-intake.md)); when in doubt, err slightly toward fresh.
