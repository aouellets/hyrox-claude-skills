# Running Development

Running fitness expressed under fatigue is the largest single determinant of
finish time for most HYROX athletes (see [coaching-principles](coaching-principles.md),
§2). This reference covers how to build the engine: aerobic base, threshold,
race-pace work, speed reserve, economy, and durability — and how that running
integrates with stations and strength without either side blunting the other.

The qualities here are general endurance-development principles, **specialized
for a race where the running is interrupted eight times and performed on
compromised legs**. The running you build here is rehearsed under fatigue in
[compromised-running](compromised-running.md).

## The training qualities

- **Aerobic base (easy volume).** The foundation. Most running volume is genuinely
  easy — conversational, ~RPE 3–4, comfortably nasal-breathing for many, roughly
  60–75% of HR max where HR is available. Easy days must stay easy; the most
  common error is running easy days at a moderate "grey-zone" pace that adds
  fatigue without adding the base adaptation.
- **Threshold.** Sustainable hard running at roughly the pace you could hold for
  ~60 minutes (~RPE 7–8, near comfortably-hard). Built with tempo blocks
  (e.g. 2 × 12–20 min) and cruise intervals (e.g. 5–6 × 5–6 min with short
  jog rest). Threshold pace is close to the effort that matters in the race.
- **Race-pace / specific work.** Repeats at goal ~1 km pace, ideally as
  run-station bricks (see [simulation-design](simulation-design.md)) rather than
  isolated track repeats — the goal is holding race pace when fatigued, not fresh.
- **Speed reserve.** A modest amount of faster-than-race running (short reps,
  strides, hill sprints) so race pace sits well below top-end. A larger gap
  between max speed and race pace makes race pace feel easier.
- **Economy.** The metabolic cost of a given pace. Improved by easy volume over
  time, strides, light plyometrics, and cadence work. Economy compounds: it is
  free speed at the same effort.
- **Durability.** The ability to hold pace and economy late in a long effort
  when fatigued. This is the race-specific quality and is trained with longer
  runs, run-heavy bricks, and the compromised-running work — not by fresh PRs.

## Example interval progressions

Progress by a defined criterion, not the calendar (see
[coaching-principles](coaching-principles.md), §5). Advance only when the prior
session was completed at target pace with the prescribed rest and RPE in range.

- **Repeat-1 km work.** 4 × 1 km at goal pace, 90 s jog → 5 × 1 km → 6 × 1 km →
  reduce rest → convert to bricks (1 km + a station). Hold pace constant; let
  volume or density advance, then specificity.
- **Threshold cruise intervals.** 4 × 5 min → 5 × 5 min → 6 × 5 min → 3 × 8 min
  → 2 × 15 min continuous tempo. Pace constant, duration up, rest down.
- **Long easy run.** Extend by ~10% steps with a down week roughly every 3–4
  weeks; back off if easy pace drifts or HR climbs at the same effort.

## Integrating running with stations and strength

Running and strength/station work compete for recovery (concurrent training,
[coaching-principles](coaching-principles.md), §4). Practical sequencing:

- Keep hard runs and heavy lower-body/sled days separated by easy days; do not
  stack a threshold session the day after heavy sleds or sandbag lunges.
- Put the highest-priority quality first in a session when fresh; never bury the
  key run under accumulated station fatigue unless the brick fatigue *is* the
  point.
- Most weeks are mostly easy running plus two quality sessions; the station and
  strength work fills the rest within the athlete's recoverable budget.

## Tracking impact exposure and avoiding spikes

Running volume, sled distance, and lunge/eccentric volume all carry impact and
tissue cost. Track them so weekly load rises gradually; avoid unjustified
spikes (a sudden jump in mileage or a first long brick after a flat block is a
classic overuse trigger). When the athlete is under-recovered, the regression is
the plan, not a failure of it. Sudden, disproportionate soreness or pain routes
to [safety-and-referral](safety-and-referral.md).
