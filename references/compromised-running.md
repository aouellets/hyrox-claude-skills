# Compromised Running

Running well immediately after a station is a distinct, trainable skill — not a
byproduct of being a good runner (see [coaching-principles](coaching-principles.md),
§3). Every run after the first in a HYROX race begins on legs and lungs degraded
by the preceding station, and the run *out of the roxzone* is where most athletes
lose the most time relative to their fresh pace. This reference covers what
changes when you run fatigued and how to train it without wrecking recovery.

## What is actually compromised

- **Legs (especially post-sled).** Heavy posterior-chain and quad work floods the
  legs; the first 100–200 m of the following run feels leaden and turnover
  collapses. Sled push, sled pull, sandbag lunges, and wall balls are the worst
  offenders for the run that follows.
- **Breathing.** After ergs and burpees the athlete leaves the station in
  oxygen debt with a high, shallow breathing pattern that has to be brought back
  under control while already moving.
- **Posture.** Carries and lunges leave the athlete flexed forward or
  laterally loaded; running tall again takes conscious effort.
- **Grip and arms.** Farmers carry, sled pull, and wall balls tax grip and
  shoulders; this does not slow the legs directly but raises overall RPE.

## The transition-to-run skill

Train the first 200 m out of every station as a deliberate sequence:

1. **Pace control first.** Resist the urge to chase normal pace immediately. The
   first 100–200 m is run a touch under target to let the system settle; trying
   to hit fresh pace off heavy legs spikes the cost and the athlete pays later.
   See [pacing-model](pacing-model.md) for the per-run targets.
2. **Cadence restoration.** Deliberately quicken turnover with short, light steps
   to re-establish rhythm even before pace returns. Cadence comes back before
   stride length does.
3. **Breathing recovery.** Impose a breathing pattern (e.g. rhythmic
   exhale-focused breathing) rather than letting it stay panicked and shallow.
4. **Postural recovery.** Actively stand tall — ribs down, eyes up — to undo the
   flexed posture left by the station.

By ~200–300 m the athlete should have settled into the planned compromised pace
for that run. The drift across the eight runs (the expected positive split) is
the subject of [pacing-model](pacing-model.md).

## How to train it

- **Run-station bricks** are the primary tool: a station immediately followed by
  a run segment, rehearsing the exact transition. Vary which station precedes the
  run so the athlete practices recovering from each demand — sled-to-run and
  lunge-to-run deserve the most reps because they cost the most.
- **Progressive fatigue exposure.** Start with one or two bricks, build the number
  and the size of the run segment over weeks (see
  [running-development](running-development.md) and
  [simulation-design](simulation-design.md)). Advance on a criterion: the athlete
  holds the target compromised pace and recovers turnover within the planned
  distance.
- **Targeted sled-to-run work.** Because the post-sled run is where time leaks
  most, a sled-push or sled-pull effort followed by a controlled 400–600 m run is
  one of the highest-value specific sessions.

## Why NOT to simulate the whole race constantly

A full simulation is the most fatiguing session an athlete does and is costly to
recover from; built too often it crowds out the easy volume and quality running
that actually builds the engine. Compromised running is trained mostly with
*bricks and segments*, reserving full simulations for sparse, deliberate
benchmark points ([simulation-design](simulation-design.md)). Turning every
session into a race burns the athlete down without a proportionate adaptation —
the opposite of specificity done well.

If post-station leg "deadness" presents as sharp, localized, or persistent pain
rather than ordinary fatigue, or if disproportionate soreness follows heavy
eccentric work, route to [safety-and-referral](safety-and-referral.md).
