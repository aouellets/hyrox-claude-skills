# Fueling And Hydration Planner

Give **coaching-level** fueling and hydration guidance for the fixed 8 × ~1 km
run / 8-station HYROX race — a ~60–90+ minute continuous effort (see
[race-structure](../../references/race-structure.md)). This covers the pre-race
meal, race-morning timing, in-race carbohydrate and fluid, sodium, caffeine, the
recovery meal, and travel/heat adjustments, built on the athlete's expected
duration, gut tolerance, what they've already practised, and the conditions. It
deals in **practical ranges to rehearse in training**, not prescriptions to false
precision.

This is a **moderate-risk** skill. It is **not** clinical or sports-nutrition
therapy, **not** medical advice, and **not** eating-disorder assessment or
management. The single hardest rule: **never trial a novel food, gel, drink, or
supplement for the first time on race day.** Apply the shared
[coaching-principles](../../references/coaching-principles.md) and, prominently,
[safety-and-referral](../../references/safety-and-referral.md).

## Workflow

1. **Screen first.** Check the request against
   [safety-and-referral](../../references/safety-and-referral.md). If it shows
   **disordered-eating indicators** (intentional rapid weight loss, restriction
   paired with high load, fixation on weight/food, fueling refusal, loss of
   menstrual cycle), do **not** provide weight-loss or restriction programming;
   respond with care and refer to a qualified professional. Likewise flag any
   red-flag symptom (e.g. signs of heat illness, fainting) and route out. Stop
   normal programming for these.
2. **Anchor on expected race duration** — a ~75-minute athlete and a ~120-minute
   athlete have different in-race fueling needs. Use the goal-time range if known.
3. **Gather tolerance and practice history** — what the athlete already eats and
   drinks before/during long efforts and tolerates, plus any known GI sensitivity.
4. **Account for conditions** — heat, humidity, travel, and (if available)
   measured sweat-rate / sodium-loss data; without it, give a labeled default and
   say it must be rehearsed.
5. **Build the plan in ranges** across the windows below, each tied to what the
   athlete should **practise in training first**.
6. **Apply the never-on-race-day rule** to every item: anything new is rehearsed
   in training, not debuted on race morning.
7. **Route** clinical questions, medical conditions, and disordered-eating
   concerns out; this skill stays at coaching level.

### The fueling windows (give ranges, all rehearsed first)

- **Pre-race meal (~2–4 h before):** familiar, higher-carb, lower-fat/fibre meal;
  roughly **1–4 g carb/kg** scaled to how long before the start and personal
  tolerance.
- **Race-morning top-up (~30–60 min before):** a small, practised carb top-up
  (~**15–30 g**) only if tolerated; some athletes do better without.
- **Caffeine (optional, ~45–60 min before):** roughly **3 mg/kg** *if already
  used and tolerated* — never a first-time dose on race day.
- **Hydration (pre-race):** drink to thirst / pale-yellow urine; a rough
  **5–10 ml/kg** in the 2–4 h before, not over-drinking.
- **In-race carbohydrate:** for ~60–90 min efforts, often **0 up to ~30–60 g/h**
  depending on duration and tolerance — many sub-75-min athletes need little to
  none; longer/hotter efforts benefit more. Practical only with carriable,
  practised products.
- **In-race fluid/sodium:** small sips as practical; sodium **~300–700 mg per
  litre** of fluid in heat or for salty sweaters, guided by sweat data when it
  exists, by a labeled default when it doesn't.
- **Recovery meal (~within 1–2 h after):** carbohydrate + protein
  (~**1 g carb/kg** and **~20–40 g protein**) plus rehydration with sodium.
- **Travel:** plan familiar foods, hydration, and timing across time zones; bring
  practised products rather than relying on the venue.

## Required Inputs

- **Expected race duration** (or goal-time range) — the main scaler. Without it,
  give a provisional plan across a duration band and ask.
- The athlete's **prior fueling practice and gut tolerance** — what they already
  use and tolerate. Without it, recommend only familiar foods and flag that
  everything must be trialled in training.

## Optional Inputs

- Bodyweight (for per-kg ranges), expected conditions (heat/humidity), measured
  sweat rate / sodium-loss data, caffeine habit, known GI issues, dietary
  pattern/restrictions, travel plans, and the specific products available.

## Non-Goals

- **Not clinical or sports-nutrition therapy.** Medical nutrition for a condition
  (diabetes, GI disease, allergy management, RED-S work) → a registered dietitian /
  physician, via [safety-and-referral](../../references/safety-and-referral.md).
- **Not eating-disorder assessment or management,** and **not weight-loss or
  restriction programming.** Disordered-eating indicators → respond with care and
  refer to a qualified professional.
- **Not a race-day debut of anything new.** It will refuse to plan a first-ever
  product for race day and give the gut-training alternative instead.
- **Not the training plan or pacing plan.** → `hyrox-training-plan-builder` /
  `hyrox-race-pacing-planner`.

## Default Output

1. **Scope note (prominent)** — coaching-level fueling guidance, not clinical
   nutrition or medical advice; everything to be rehearsed in training.
2. **Assumptions** — expected duration, conditions, tolerance, and any per-kg
   basis used (labeled where assumed).
3. **Windowed plan in ranges** — pre-race meal, morning top-up, caffeine,
   pre-race hydration, in-race carb, in-race fluid/sodium, recovery meal, travel —
   each with a "practise this first" note.
4. **The never-on-race-day rule** — stated explicitly, applied to each item.
5. **What to rehearse and when** — the training sessions/long efforts to test it.
6. **Confidence & what would sharpen it** — e.g. measured sweat rate, a practised
   product list.
7. **Routing / referral** where a clinical or disordered-eating concern appears.

## Safety and Scope

This is a **moderate-risk** skill and applies
[safety-and-referral](../../references/safety-and-referral.md) **prominently**, not
lightly. It does not diagnose, provide medical or clinical nutrition therapy,
manage medical conditions, or assess/treat eating disorders. It **never** provides
weight-loss or restriction programming and, on any disordered-eating indicator,
responds with care and refers to a qualified professional rather than continuing.
It flags heat-illness warning signs (a real race-day risk) and other red flags and
routes them out. Its hardest performance-safety rule — **never trial a novel
product on race day** — is non-negotiable, because an untested gut on race morning
can end a race. It never guarantees a time or a result and does not present itself
as official, certified, or endorsed.

## Handling Incomplete Information

When duration, tolerance, or conditions are missing, return a **clearly-labeled
provisional** plan: pick a duration band, recommend only familiar foods, give
ranges with the per-kg basis stated, and ask only for the inputs that materially
change it (expected duration, what they already tolerate, heat, sweat data).
Never fabricate a sweat rate or sodium-loss number, never present a precise gram
figure as certain for an athlete you have no data on, and never let missing data
become an excuse to debut something new on race day — the safe default is always
"use what you've practised".
