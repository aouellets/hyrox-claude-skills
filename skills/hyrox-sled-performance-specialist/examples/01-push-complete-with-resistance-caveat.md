# Example 1 — Complete data: sled-push build, with the gym-vs-race resistance caveat

**User:**
> Coach my sled push. I push 4 plates (~100 kg added) on the turf at my gym for
> 25 m in about 22 s but I stall and the run after is a disaster. I race in 9 weeks,
> back squat 160 kg, train on heavy artificial turf. How heavy is the race sled and
> what should I be training at?

**Response:**

## Sled context & the resistance caveat (read this first)
You asked "how heavy is the race sled" — I won't give you a number from memory.
**The race sled load is season- and division-dependent**; it comes from the current
rules registry, not from me, so I'd confirm it via
`hyrox-division-and-rules-advisor` for your season/division.

More importantly: **the plate count on your gym sled is not the resistance you'll
feel on race day.** Your "~100 kg on heavy artificial turf" could feel *heavier or
lighter* than the race sled depending on the race surface, the sled model, friction
and wear, and calibration. Even matching the race plate count wouldn't reproduce
race feel if the surface differs. So everything below is prescribed in **effort and
your own surface**, and any race-resistance comparison is a labeled assumption with
a wide band — re-calibrate by feel if you ever push on a different surface.

## Limiter read
Strength is not your limiter (160 kg squat is ample). The stall + ruined run points
to **posture breakdown and surge-and-stall pacing**, plus untrained **sled-to-run**.
Confidence: medium-high (good numbers, one clear pattern).

## Push coaching
- **Start:** build tension, drive the first 3–4 steps *hard* to beat inertia, then
  settle — don't blow the whole effort in the first surge.
- **Posture / body angle:** low, long line from hands to heels, arms as rigid
  struts. Your stall is the classic upright-torso force leak — keep hips down.
- **Hand position:** consistent height that lets you keep the lean; brace it.
- **Footwork:** short, choppy, powerful, *continuous* — no stop-start.
- **Sustained drive vs acceleration:** hard out of the start, then one continuous
  controlled drive rather than repeated max surges that cook the legs.

## Strength block (background, not the target)
- Back squat 4×4 @ RPE 7–8, heavy short sled pushes 5×15 m near-max effort (your
  surface) once/week. Maintenance, not the focus.

## Repeatability & sled-to-run (the focus)
| Wk | Session | Criterion | Regression |
|----|---------|-----------|------------|
| 1–3 | 5×(25 m push @ ~22 s effort + 200 m run @ controlled compromised pace), 2 min rest | Run turnover recovers by 150 m | drop to 4 reps |
| 4–6 | 4×(25 m push + 400 m run) at race effort | Hold compromised pace, no stall | shorten run to 300 m |
| 7–8 | 3×(50 m push split as you'd race it + 400–600 m run) | Push stays continuous, run controlled | fewer reps |

Loads tied to *your* surface and effort, not a race weight. On a different surface,
re-find the effort, don't copy the plate count.

## Proficiency test, benchmark & retest
- **Test now:** 3×25 m push on your turf, log times + how the legs feel + run-out
  cadence recovery distance.
- **Benchmark (Wk 7):** push effort + 600 m controlled run, holding compromised
  pace — this is the real skill.
- **Retest:** end of Wk 6; target no stall and a shorter cadence-recovery distance.

## Integration & adjustment
Keep the heavy sled day away from your hardest run day. Confirm the race load via
the rules advisor. For the run-out specifically, also see
`hyrox-compromised-running-coach`.

*Scope note: ordinary post-sled leg deadness is the trained target; progress load
gradually — severe disproportionate soreness/swelling or dark urine is a referral.*
